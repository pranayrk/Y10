Solid

  • The distance between the particles is small, so solids have a high density and are incompressible.
  • As they are close together, the particles in solids have strong attractive forces between them.
  • That is why solids have a fixed volume, a fixed shape and cannot flow.

Liquid

  • The distance between the particles in liquids is slightly greater than in solids and the attractive forces are not as strong.
  • That is why liquids are slightly less dense but are still incompressible and have a fixed volume.
  • That is why liquids can flow, and do not have a fixed shape but take the shape of their container.

Gas

  • The distance between the particles in gases is much greater.
  • That is why gases have low density and are compressible.
  • When a solid is heated, it melts into a liquid at its melting point.
  • A liquid that is heated will boil and become a gas at its boiling point.
  • The Kinetic theory of matter says that matter is made of tiny particles constantly in motion.
  • Particles have kinetic energy because they are moving or vibrating
  • A key piece of evidence that was used to prove the kinetic theory of matter is Brownian motion
  • Brownian motion refers to the random movement of microscopic particles in a fluid due to the collisions by the molecules of the fluid.
  • Examples of microscopic particles are pollen grains and smoke particles.
  • We can only see microscopic particles under the microscope as the molecules are too small to be seen.
Explanation of Brownian Motion
Air consists mainly of nitrogen molecules $N_2$, and oxygen molecules $O_2$. These molecules are too small to be seen under the microscope. A smoke particle is a solid lump of many carbon atoms. When light is shone on a mixture of smoke particles in air, the smoke particles can be seen as tiny specks of reflected light. There are millions of molecules in the air moving at high speeds in all directions. This means that there are many collisions on each smoke particle happening all the time. The smoke particle is constantly pushed one way and then another. As we cannot see the molecules, the smoke particles appear to be constantly moving small distances in a random path.
  • To observe Brownian motion in the lab, we use a smoke cell which is a transparent box with smoke trapped inside
  • Shining a light through the cell and observing it under a microscope, we can see the smoke particles as tiny bright specks of light following a zig-zag random motion.
  • When an object is heated, the temperature of the object increases. This means that the average kinetic energy of the particles in the object increases and the particles move or vibrate faster.
  • When an object is cooled, the temperature of the object decreases. This means that the average kinetic energy of the particles in the object decreases and the particles move more slowly.
  • The lowest temperature where the particles have the least kinetic energy occurs at –273°C.
  • This temperature is also known as absolute zero.
