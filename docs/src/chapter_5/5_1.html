### 5.1: Moving particles The kinetic model of matter can be used to explain many physical properties of matter: It can be used to explain why: * Liquids take up the shape of their container * Gases fill their container * Solids keep their shape * Gases diffuse (spread out) from place to place * Most solids expand when they melt.
### 5.1: Moving particles * It can also be used to explain the nature of **heat** * The particles of solids and liquids are in constant motion. * When heated they vibrate faster, so force each other a little further apart * Kinetic theory uses the idea that as particles heat up **they gain more kinetic energy and move faster**.
### 5.1: Moving particles * **Heat** is energy that is transferred from one body to another due to a difference in temperature * Heat transfer increases the energy of the colder object by transferring kinetic energy from the hotter object

How is temperature measured?

### 5.1: Moving Particles ##### Celsius Scale * The Celsius scale was devised by the Swedish scientist **Anders Celsius**. * It has two fixed points: * $0^\circ C$: the melting point of pure ice at atmospheric pressure * $100^\circ C$: the boiling point of pure water at atmospheric pressure.

5.1: Moving Particles

### 5.1: Moving Particles * When a thermometer is used to measure the temperature of a body it has to come into contact with the body. * A **thermal interaction** takes place and energy is transferred until the thermometer and the body are at the same temperature. * When this happens we say that we have **thermal equilibrium.**
### 5.1: Moving Particles > As a substance cools down its **particles lose kinetic energy and slow down**. * If the particles lose all their kinetic energy and stop moving, it is not possible for the substance to cool any further. * Kinetic theory suggests there is a limit to how low temperatures can go. * The lowest possible temperature is around −273 °C. * This is called **absolute zero**.
### 5.1: Moving Particles ##### Kelvin Scale * The Kelvin temperature scale starts from absolute zero, or −273 °C. > $$\text{temp in K} = \text{temp in $^\circ$C} + 273.15$$
