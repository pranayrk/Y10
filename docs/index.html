Testing the Reflection of Light
  • Mark out a dotted line perpendicular to the mirror on the paper. This line is the called the normal.
  • Label the intersection of the mirror and the normal as $O$.
  • Switch on the ray box and direct a ray of light at point $O$.
  • Measure and record the angle of incidence $i$ and the corresponding angle of reflection $r$.
### Observations * The image is of the *same size* as the object. * The image is *laterally inverted*. The left-hand side of the image appears as the right-hand side of the object and vice versa. * The image is *upright*. * The image has the *same distance* from the mirror as the object.

Law of Reflection: The angle of incidence is equal to the angle to reflection.

Testing the Refraction of Light
  • Place the glass block on a piece of paper.
  • Vary the angle of incidence $i$ and measure the corresponding angles of refraction $r$.

Observations

  • A light ray that travels from one medium to another along the normal is not refracted.
  • A light ray bends towards the normal when it enters an optically denser medium at an angle (air to glass).
  • A light ray bends away from the normal when it enters an optically less dense medium at an angle (glass to air).
  • The incident ray, the normal and the refracted ray all lie in the same plane.

Law of Refraction or Snell's Law

$$ \frac{\sin{i}}{\sin{r}} = \text{constant}$$
### Refractive Index * Consider light travelling through a medium. The refractive index $n$ of the medium is defined as $n=\displaystyle \frac{\text{speed of light in vacuum}}{\text{speed of light in medium}} = \displaystyle \frac{c}{v}$ * The *higher* the refractive index of a medium, the *slower* light travels in the medium. * For light travelling *from a vacuum to an optical medium*, the refractive index $n$ of the medium can also be defined as $\\;n = \displaystyle \frac{\sin(i)}{\sin(r)}$
**Question:** Given that the speed of light in vacuum is $3.0 \times 10^8 m/s$, calculate the speed of light in crown glass of refractive index $1.52$.

Notice that the speed of light in vacuum is close to the speed of light in air.

So we often approximate the refractive index $n$ of a material as $$n= \frac{\text{speed of light in vacuum}}{\text{speed of light in medium}} \approx \frac{\text{speed of light in air}}{\text{speed of light in medium}}$$

A light ray will travel along the same path if its direction of travel is reversed.

This is the principle of reversibility and it applies to the reflection and refraction of light.
